Exploring the Relationship Between the Microbiome-Gut-Brain-Axis and Major Depressive Disorder

Introduction

According to the DSM-V, Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) is a debilitating and prevalent disorder that is characterized by persistent feelings of sadness and/or anhedonia over the span of at least two weeks. An estimated 5.7% of adults worldwide suffer from depression1, and yet it remains poorly understood due to its elusive pathogenesis and heterogeneous etiology, i.e. it arises due to multiple complex and interacting factors. Depression has been found to have moderate heritability2, which has made it necessary to investigate other potential biological markers. Some reviews of pooled trials with placebo-controlled groups have found that the difference between antidepressants and placebos on standard symptom scales is small enough to be clinically insignificant despite being statistically significant3. This has prompted researchers to explore alternative pathways to treatment through the analysis of molecular biomarkers of depression.

The microbiota-gut-brain-axis (MGBA) refers to the bidirectional network (i.e. two-way communication system) between the gut microbiota, the gastrointestinal tract, and the brain (i.e. the central nervous system). This network relies on metabolic, endocrine, neural, and immunological pathways, including the vagus nerve, the enteric nervous system (ENS), the Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis, bacterial metabolites, and mediators of the immune system (e.g. cytokines)45. Evidence suggests that the dysfunction within this network may play a role in psychiatric disorders by influencing immune, endocrine, and neural regulatory processes5.

In this independent literature review, I aim to provide an overview of our current understanding of the potential link between microbiota-gut-brain-axis and Major Depressive Disorder through qualitative and quantitative analysis of several selected papers while noting limitations.

Qualitative Findings Across Studies

Several converging findings were reported across the selected literature. Notably, gut microbiota perturbations do not appear to be specific to single diagnoses but rather show transdiagnostic overlap between MDD, schizophrenia, psychosis and bipolar disorder710. Furthermore, several psychiatric disorders, including MDD, are associated with an increase in abundance of pro-inflammatory bacteria, with the genus Eggerthella being the most consistently enriched710. This effect coincided with a reduction in anti-inflammatory, butyrate-producing bacteria, specifically the genera Faecalibacterium and Coprococcus610. The associated gut bacteria are linked to the synthesis and regulation of key neurotransmitters, including glutamate, butyrate, serotonin, and gamma amino butyric acid (GABA)610. Additionally, patients with MDD showed a decrease in GMV in distributed cortical and subcortical regions68; specifically, Lactococcus chungangensis was found to be negatively correlated with the GMV of the left IFG and Streptococcus gallolyticus with the GMV of left cuneus, left pallidum, and left inferior parietal lobule (IPL)6.

Besides this, the pilot study also identified 94 differential species between the MDD and the healthy control groups — specifically, increased abundance ofRuminococcaceae, Streptococcaceae, Lactobacillaceae, and Bifidobacteriaceae, while Lachnospiraceae and Prevotellaceae were decreased. The gut microbiome in MDD subjects also showed an increased abundance of genes associated with galactose, starch, and sucrose metabolism. Along the same vein, the study further found 34 significantly different metabolites, with 29 increased and 5 decreased in the MDD group. The most predominantly increased metabolites were lipids (such as phosphoethanolamine (PEA)), nucleotides and vitamins (such as nicotinamide and Vitamin A), whereas amino acids were found to be diminished. Additionally, the study highlights the NLRP3 inflammasome as a potential vital link between lipid metabolism and inflammation as the NLRP3 inflammasome leads to the production of IL-1β, a specific inflammatory cytokine elevated in patients with MDD. The authors hypothesize that NLRP3 inflammasome activity in the prefrontal cortex (IFG) may be a mechanism through which gut microbiota and lipid disturbances contribute to MDD.

Another large association study (n = 2,593) highlights the genus Sellimonas as one of its most significant findings, nothing its positive correlation with depressive symptoms and its increase in abundance following dysbiosis, suggesting it may thrive in perturbed gut environments7. Moreover, the genus Hungatella (known to produce the precursor for trimethylamine-N-oxide (TMAO), a molecule implicated in neurological disease) was also found to be positively correlated with depressive symptoms, alongside Eggerthella, Lachnoclostridium and Sellimonas as mentioned previously. The study also mentions the taxa Subdoligranulum, Coprococcus, Eubacterium ventriosum, Ruminococcus gauvreauii group, Lachnospiraceae UCG-001, and several groups within the Ruminococcaceae family (UCG002, UCG003, UCG005).

Some studies discuss novel treatments of MDD via targeting the MGBA, as approximately 60% of patients experience some degree of nonresponse to first-line treatments and one-third continue experiencing symptoms despite further intervention9. Multi-strain probiotics, when administered adjunctively to standard medication, have been shown to be effective in patients with an incomplete response89. The strains include species such as Bacillus subtilis, Bifidobacterium (bifidum, breve, infantis, longum), Lactobacillus (acidophilus, casei, plantarum, helveticus, etc.), Lactococcus lactis, and Streptococcus thermophilus89. Successful treatment, notably, is often accompanied by an increase in the genus Lactobacillus8. In a randomized clinical trial, patients receiving 8 weeks of adjunctive probiotics showed significantly greater improvements in symptoms (measured by HAMD-17 and IDS scores) compared to the placebo group — experiencing, on average, a reduction of one on both scales9. This intervention is supported by its high acceptability and well-tolerance, with one study reporting adherence rates of 97.2% and low attrition rates of approximately 8%9. Furthermore, probiotic supplementation was found to maintain and stabilize microstructural integrity of the uncinate fasciculus (a white-matter tract connecting the limbic system to the frontal lobe), possibly counteracting or preventing the neuronal degeneration associated with MDD8.


Quantitative Findings Across Studies

The genus Eggerthella is consistently enriched in patients with MDD, appearing as a significant finding in 10 out of the 11 studies analyzed in a large meta-analysis and confirmed in a 2,593-person study710. In one meta-analysis, the genus Faecalibacterium and Coprococcus are consistently depleted in patients with MDD and psychiatric populations across 15 out of 17 and 10 out of 10 studies respectively reporting the genera10. Other shared microbial patterns include the enrichment of Lactobacillus (10 out of 13 studies), Enterococcus (8 of 9 studies) and Streptococcus (8 out of 10 studies) across MDD and other psychiatric disorders.

In biostatistics, alpha diversity refers to the species richness found in a small, specific ecosystem. There are several metrics used to measure the alpha diversity of a sample, most notably the Shannon index, the Simpson index, and the Chao1 index. The Shannon Index (H) is an ecological formula or statistic used to measure species diversity within a community by accounting for both species richness and evenness, with values typically ranging between 1.5 and 3.5. A value of 0 means the ecosystem has only one species. Simpson's Index (D) is a formula used to measure the biodiversity of a habitat by calculating the likelihood that two randomly selected individuals belong to the same species. The values range from 0 to 1, as with standard probability, with a value near 0 corresponding to a high species diversity and a value near 1 corresponding to the opposite. The Chao1 index is a nonparametric method used to estimate the true species richness of a community. Higher the value, higher the biodiversity. On the other hand, the beta diversity is a measure of how species composition changes across different sites, habitats, or ecosystems, with values ranging from 0 to 1 and 0 meaning the communities are identical. These statistical metrics will be further explored in dissection.

In the selected literature, one meta-analysis reported that higher levels of depressive symptoms were significantly associated with lower alpha diversity, specifically the Shannon index, across a large discovery cohort (beta = −1.57, p = 1.5 × 10⁻³) and a large validation cohort (beta = −0.64, p = 2.84 × 10⁻²)7. Another meta-analysis of 34 studies observed a standardized mean difference (SMD) of -0.26 for observed species and -0.5 for the Chao1 index, suggesting a significant decrease in microbial richness in psychiatric patients10. Regarding beta diversity, the same analysis showed that in 12 out of 15 studies, patients with MDD clustered differently from controls, showing a distinct phylogenetic structure. Adjunctive probiotic treatment produced significant standardized effect sizes (SES) of 0.70 for improving depressive symptoms (HAMD-17) and 0.67 for clinician-rated anxiety (HAMA)9. In the same study, probiotics continued to show efficacy by the eighth week with an SES of 0.64 for depressive symptoms (IDS-SR) and 0.79 for anxiety symptoms (HAMA). On average, participants in probiotic arms experienced a reduction of one severity grade on depression rating scales over 8 weeks.

Very notably, one study suggested a causal link between Eggerthella and MDD7. Specifically, the study used Mendelian Randomization analysis (MR) (a method that uses genetic variants (SNPs) as natural random experiments (similar to RCTs) to infer causality between an exposure and an outcome) and found that MDD may causally increase the abundance of Eggerthella (effect = 0.237, p = 0.027), meaning higher genetically predicted depression risk is associated with higher levels of this genus. A second MR analysis suggests Sellimonas abundance may reduce depression risk (effect = −0.046>, p> = 5.5×10⁻⁴), though this conflicted with their observational findings. Tests showed no heterogenity or horizontal pleiotropy, meaning the genetic instruments likely affected the outcome mainly through the exposure. In another study, researchers trained a Random Forest model (a machine-learning algorithm that builds many decision trees and combines them) to classify whether someone has major depressive disorder based on gut bacteria6. The model achieved 98% accuracy (AUC = 0.98) in predicting an MDD diagnosis using only six specific bacterial species. The same study further identified 34 significantly different metabolites in MDD patients, with 29 increased (mostly lipids and vitamins) and 5 decreased (mostly amino acids). Their analysis further yielded 94 differential bacterial species discriminating the gut microbiota in patients with MDD and the healthy control group, among which 69.15% belonged to Firmicutes, 18.09% to Bacteroidetes and 11.70% to Actinobacteria. Moreover, MDD patients exhibited significantly higher plasma IL−1β levels (858.30 ± 432.70 pg/ml) compared to healthy controls (359.52 ± 160.63 pg/ml, p < 0.001).

In one probiotic study, a factorial ANOVA (statistical test comparing group differences across multiple factors) found significant effects of treatment group and brain hemisphere on fractional anisotropy (FA) in the uncinate fasciculus8. Specifically, factorial ANOVA showed significant main effects of group (F = 7.433, p = 0.007) and hemisphere (F = 7.288, p = 0.008), i.e. the probiotic group had higher FA than the placebo group, suggesting improved or preserved white-matter integrity, and FA was also higher in the left hemisphere than the right overall. A significant time-by-group interaction (indicating that change over time differed between probiotic and placebo groups) (F = 6.303, p = 0.013) was found for the right UF. Mean diffusivity (MD) (a neuroimaging parameter that measures the overall magnitude of water diffusion in brain tissue, with higher MD often indicating reduced tissue integrity) increased in the placebo group but remained stable in the control group, implying that probiotic supplementation may have prevented structural degeneration. Additionally, Symptom improvement correlated strongly with FA increases (rho = −0.662, p = 0.010) and MD decreases (rho = 0.701, p = 0.005) in the left UF within the probiotic group. In another probiotic study, exploratory analysis of 9-item Anxiety/Arousal subscale showed a significant standardized effect size (SES) of 0.75 for the probiotic group, suggesting that the reduction in depression scale ratings may be driven by the reduction in anxious and somatic symptoms9.


Limitations

Three of the five included studies had a small sample size, (n ≤ 50 ) and one acknowledged that it may limit the validity of their statistical assessment as well as the generalization and precision of their findings689. Additionally, the two included meta-analyses also reported modest primary sample sizes alongside the lack of diverse studies710. One meta-analysis reported the inconsistency in stool sampling methods and different sequencing regions, which may significantly influence the resulting microbial profiles7. Many psychiatric medications, including antidepressants, are known to alter the gut microbiome710. While one study used drug-naive patients, the others include both medicated and unmedicated participants, making it difficult to separate "illness effects" from "medication effects"810. Furthermore, it has been found that diet and regional variation profoundly affect microbiota composition, but few studies record detailed dietary information or adequately account for smoking or alcohol consumption610.

The research is also heavily clustered around specific age-groups, regions, and ethnicities, making it difficult to generalize findings for underrepresented groups710. One pilot study could not clarify whether the observed changes in the gut microbiota were the cause of depression or the consequence of it, and conceded that the multi-omics correlations and the proposed NLRP3 inflammasome hypothesis were not verified through animal experiments6. In the two studies concerned with treatment, one noted that an additional follow-up MRI session would have been advantageous, as the clinical antidepressant effects were strongest at the 8-week mark but brain scans only occurred at 4 weeks8. In the other study, researchers could not determine if the positive effects of probiotics were due to their interaction with SSRIs or if they would be generalizable to other treatments9. Adherence was also measured via "capsule count", which is the standard method but is known to lead potential overreporting by participants.

The studies also reported some statistical limitations. One meta-analysis reported a possible loss in statistical power due to the use of different depression scale ratings from its selected literature7. The same study further conceded that the Mendelian Randomization analysis (a method that uses genetic variants as natural, random and unconfounded proxies to determine if a specific factor causes a health outcome) conducted lacked power because the individual genetic variants for depression have very small effects (odds ratio < 1.05), making it unlikely they would show strong associations with the microbiome. Moreover, the taxonomy was assigned using closed reference OTU clustering (a method that maps sequencing reads directly to a pre-defined, known reference database), but this method did not include a de-noising step, which led to some discrepancies between their findings and those of other cohorts, although ultimately, the researchers still observed reproducible associations for certain taxa (e.g. Lachnoclostridium, Coprococcus and Ruminoccocaceae.


Conclusion

From the synthesized literature, MDD was shown to be consistently characterized by the depletion of anti-inflammatory, butyrate-producing bacteria, specifically the genera Faecalibacterium and Coprococcus, alongside an enrichment in pro-inflammatory bacteria, most notably the genus Eggerthella, which demonstrated a potential causal link with depression. Furthermore, the NLRP3 inflammasome may serve as a potential biological link connecting lipid metabolism disorders to systemic inflammation (increased IL-1β) observed in the gut-brain axis. The gut microbiome was shown to influence the brain's physical state, with associations found between specific microbial taxa and changes in grey matter volume (GMV) and white matter integrity in distributed cortical and subcortical regions and within fronto-limbic networks. More specifically, species like Lactococcus chungangensis are negatively correlated with the GMV of the left inferior frontal gyrus (IFG) in MDD patients.

In that same vein, probiotic supplementation was shown to preserve the microstructural integrity of the uncinate fasciculus, potentially preventing neurodegeneration. Exploratory analysis suggests that the clinical improvement seen with probiotics may be specifically driven by the alleviation of anxious and somatic symptoms that are often resistant to standard antidepressants. Finally, probiotics demonstrated an exceptionally high adherence rate of 97.2%, making them a highly acceptable intervention for patients with MDD.

Personal Experience

I think the biggest thing that stood out to me is how easily you can branch into other topics upon starting. Biology is inherently complex and intricately interlinked. During this process, I came across an interesting paper which noted a potential distinction between the gut microbiota of infants born naturally and that of infants born via cesarean section. This led me to wonder what other factors the C-section alters, or more broadly, how these seemingly minor biological tweaks can impact an organism's biological profile. I am a big fan of the butterfly effect and the concept of "emergence", and I think this has been instrumental in driving my curiousity about the world around me.

While I did initially struggle to grasp some of the terminology used (intricate anatomy or statistical concepts that I am unfamiliar with), upon some additional reading I was able to discern the content with ease. I plan on dissecting said concepts in the future of this website!

I think I have found myself with more questions than when I started, and in my book, that's always a good thing. :) Hopefully in my next project I will be able to further branch out and hone in!
